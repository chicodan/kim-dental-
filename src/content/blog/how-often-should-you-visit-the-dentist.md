"Twice a year." You've heard it your whole life. But where did that advice come from, and is it still the right answer?

The truth is, **how often you should see the dentist depends on you** — your oral health history, your risk factors, and your overall health. The twice-yearly guideline is a good starting point for many people, but it's not a universal rule.

Here's a more nuanced look at what the evidence says — and how we help patients at Kim Dental build the right care schedule for their individual needs.

## Where "Twice a Year" Comes From

The six-month cleaning interval dates back to the mid-20th century and was largely popularized by a toothpaste company's marketing campaigns. Dentists have debated its scientific basis ever since. What we do know is:

- Professional cleanings remove tartar (hardened plaque) that brushing and flossing cannot
- Regular checkups catch problems early — when they're smaller, cheaper, and easier to treat
- The right interval varies significantly by patient

## The Low-Risk Patient: Once a Year May Be Enough

If you have excellent oral hygiene, no history of cavities or gum disease, healthy gums, and no significant medical conditions that affect oral health — one annual visit may be sufficient.

Signs you may be lower risk:
- No new cavities in the past several years
- Gums don't bleed when you floss
- No family history of aggressive gum disease
- You brush twice daily and floss consistently

Even if you're low-risk, skipping visits entirely is never recommended. Problems can develop silently — oral cancer, for instance, often shows no early pain.

## The Higher-Risk Patient: 3–4 Visits Per Year

Some patients genuinely need more frequent professional care. We typically recommend quarterly visits (every 3–4 months) for patients who:

- Have a history of gum disease (periodontitis)
- Are prone to cavities despite good hygiene
- Are pregnant (hormonal changes increase gum inflammation risk)
- Have diabetes — which both worsens and is worsened by gum disease
- Smoke or use tobacco products
- Have dry mouth from medications or health conditions
- Are undergoing chemotherapy or radiation
- Wear braces or complex dental appliances
- Have a history of oral cancer

## Children: Start Early, Visit Often

We recommend bringing children in for their first dental visit by age one — or when the first tooth appears. Early visits build positive associations with dental care and allow us to monitor development closely.

Children are typically seen every six months. Those prone to cavities (a genetic tendency) may benefit from more frequent fluoride treatments and sealants.

## What Happens During a Routine Visit?

A standard checkup at Kim Dental includes:
- **Professional cleaning** — removing plaque and tartar above and below the gumline
- **Oral exam** — checking for cavities, gum disease, and abnormal tissue
- **Oral cancer screening** — a quick visual and tactile check of lips, tongue, cheeks, and throat
- **X-rays** — typically once a year to catch decay between teeth and bone level changes

The whole appointment usually takes 45–60 minutes for a healthy patient.

## What Happens When You Skip Visits?

Small problems get big. A tiny cavity that costs $150 to fill becomes a root canal ($800–1,500) and then a crown ($1,200–1,800) if ignored long enough. Gum disease that could be reversed with more frequent cleanings progresses silently until bone is lost — and bone cannot be regenerated.

Preventive care is always the least expensive, least invasive path.
