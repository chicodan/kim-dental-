When it comes to wisdom teeth, there's a common assumption that everyone needs to have them removed eventually. But is that really true? The answer might surprise you. While many people do benefit from wisdom tooth extraction, it's not always a one-size-fits-all situation. Let's explore what wisdom teeth are, why they sometimes cause problems, and how to know if yours need attention.

## What Are Wisdom Teeth and Why Do We Have Them?

Wisdom teeth are your third set of molars, typically appearing between ages 17 and 25. Our ancestors needed these extra teeth to help chew a diet of rough, uncooked foods. Today, our diets have changed significantly, and our jaws have actually become smaller over generations. This evolutionary shift means there's often **not enough room** in our mouths for these late arrivals.

Most people develop four wisdom teeth—one in each corner of the mouth—but some people have fewer, and others don't develop any at all. Interestingly, about **35% of people** are born without one or more wisdom teeth, which is perfectly normal.

## When Wisdom Teeth Become Problematic

So when do wisdom teeth actually need to come out? There are several situations where extraction becomes the recommended course of action:

**Impaction** is one of the most common reasons for removal. This occurs when a wisdom tooth doesn't have enough space to emerge properly and becomes trapped beneath the gum line or grows in at an awkward angle. Impacted teeth can cause pain, swelling, and even damage to neighboring teeth.

**Crowding** is another concern. Even if wisdom teeth emerge fully, they can push against your other teeth, potentially undoing years of orthodontic work or causing misalignment.

**Decay and gum disease** affect wisdom teeth more frequently than other molars because their location at the back of the mouth makes them harder to clean properly. Food particles and bacteria can easily accumulate, leading to cavities or **periodontal issues**.

**Cysts or tumors**, while rare, can develop around impacted wisdom teeth and cause damage to the jawbone and surrounding structures.

## When Keeping Your Wisdom Teeth Makes Sense

Here's the good news: **not everyone needs their wisdom teeth removed**. If your wisdom teeth have fully emerged, are positioned correctly, can be cleaned effectively, and aren't causing any pain or dental problems, there may be no reason to extract them.

During regular dental checkups, we monitor wisdom teeth development through examinations and X-rays. This ongoing observation helps us determine whether your wisdom teeth are healthy and functional or heading toward trouble.

Many of our patients here in Paramus and throughout Bergen County are relieved to learn that their wisdom teeth are doing just fine. Others appreciate catching potential problems early, before they become painful or complicated.

## A Practical Tip You Can Use Today

If you still have your wisdom teeth, pay **extra attention** to cleaning them during your daily oral hygiene routine. Use a soft-bristled toothbrush angled toward the back of your mouth, and don't skip flossing behind your last molars. Consider using an antimicrobial mouthwash to reach areas your brush might miss. If you notice any persistent pain, swelling, or difficulty opening your mouth fully, make a note to discuss these symptoms at your next dental visit.

## The Bottom Line

The decision to remove wisdom teeth should be based on your individual situation, not assumptions. Factors like the position of your teeth, your oral health history, your age, and the presence of any symptoms all play a role in determining the best approach for you.

The key is **proactive monitoring** rather than waiting until problems develop. Regular dental visits allow us to track your wisdom teeth and make recommendations based on what we actually see, not guesswork.
