Do you ever wake up with a sore jaw, a dull headache, or teeth that feel sensitive for no apparent reason? You might be one of the millions of Americans who grind their teeth—often without even realizing it. **Teeth grinding, medically known as bruxism**, is surprisingly common and can happen during the day or while you sleep. The good news is that once you understand what's causing it, there are effective ways to protect your teeth and find relief.

## What Causes Teeth Grinding?

Bruxism doesn't have a single cause, which is why it affects people of all ages. For many patients here in Bergen County, **stress and anxiety** are the primary culprits. When we're under pressure—whether from work, family responsibilities, or daily life—our bodies often hold that tension in the jaw muscles, leading to clenching and grinding, especially at night.

Other common causes include:

- **Sleep disorders** like sleep apnea, which disrupts normal sleep patterns
- **Misaligned teeth or an abnormal bite** that puts uneven pressure on your jaw
- **Certain medications**, particularly some antidepressants
- **Lifestyle factors** such as caffeine, alcohol, or tobacco use
- **Concentration habits**—many people clench their jaw while focusing intently on tasks

Children can also grind their teeth, often as their jaws develop, though most outgrow it naturally.

## The Damage Bruxism Can Cause

What makes teeth grinding particularly concerning is that many people don't know they're doing it until damage has already occurred. **Chronic bruxism can lead to serious dental problems** that require extensive treatment to repair.

Over time, grinding wears down the enamel—the hard, protective outer layer of your teeth. This can result in:

- **Flattened, chipped, or fractured teeth**
- Increased tooth sensitivity to hot and cold
- Loose teeth or damaged dental restorations
- **TMJ (temporomandibular joint) disorders** causing jaw pain, clicking, or difficulty opening your mouth
- Chronic headaches, earaches, and facial pain
- Disrupted sleep for you and your partner

During routine exams, Dr. Kim often spots the telltale signs of grinding—worn tooth surfaces, small cracks, or jaw tenderness—before patients even connect their symptoms to bruxism.

## How to Stop Grinding and Protect Your Smile

The most effective treatment for bruxism depends on what's causing it, but there are several strategies that can help.

**A custom night guard** is often the first line of defense. Unlike store-bought options, a professionally fitted night guard from your dentist is designed specifically for your mouth. It creates a protective barrier between your upper and lower teeth, preventing damage while you sleep and reducing strain on your jaw muscles.

**Stress management techniques** can also make a significant difference. Consider incorporating relaxation practices into your routine:

- Deep breathing exercises before bed
- Limiting screen time in the evening
- Gentle jaw stretches and massage
- Regular physical activity to release tension

**Here's a practical tip you can try today:** Throughout the day, practice keeping your lips together, teeth apart, and your tongue resting gently on the roof of your mouth. This "rest position" helps train your jaw muscles to relax and can reduce daytime clenching habits.

Additionally, cutting back on caffeine and alcohol—especially in the evening—can help minimize nighttime grinding. If you suspect a sleep disorder might be involved, it's worth discussing this with your healthcare provider.
