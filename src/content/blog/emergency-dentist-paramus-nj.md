A cracked tooth at dinner. A knocked-out tooth at a weekend soccer game. A throbbing toothache that kept you up all night. Dental emergencies happen at the worst possible moments — and knowing what to do in those first critical minutes can make the difference between saving a tooth and losing it.

## What Counts as a Dental Emergency?

Not every tooth problem is an emergency, but the following situations require prompt attention:

**Severe toothache**
Intense, persistent pain — especially pain that wakes you up or doesn't respond to over-the-counter pain relievers — often signals an infection or abscess. Left untreated, infections can spread rapidly. Call us immediately.

**Knocked-out tooth**
This is a true time-sensitive emergency. If a permanent tooth is knocked out, you have roughly 30–60 minutes to save it. Here's what to do:
- Handle the tooth by the crown (top), never the root
- If dirty, gently rinse with water — do not scrub
- Try to place it back in the socket, or store it in milk or between your cheek and gum
- Get to a dentist within an hour

**Cracked or fractured tooth**
A cracked tooth can be painless initially but may expose the inner pulp to bacteria, leading to infection. Any crack visible to the eye — or a sudden sharp pain when biting — warrants same-day evaluation.

**Lost filling or crown**
While not always painful, a lost restoration leaves tooth structure exposed and vulnerable. Call us so we can fit you in quickly and protect the tooth.

**Abscess or swelling**
Facial swelling, especially near the jaw, gums, or under the eye, can indicate a spreading infection. This is a medical emergency — seek care immediately, even if it means going to an emergency room before seeing us.

**Broken orthodontic appliance or wire**
If a wire from braces is poking your cheek, call us. We can often resolve these quickly without a full appointment.

## What Is NOT an Emergency

- A mild toothache that comes and goes
- A chipped tooth with no pain or rough edge
- A lost filling with no sensitivity

These situations should be addressed soon, but they can typically wait for a regular appointment.

## How to Manage Pain Before Your Appointment

While you wait to be seen:

- **Over-the-counter pain relievers** (ibuprofen, acetaminophen) can help manage discomfort
- **Cold compress** (wrapped in a cloth) applied to the cheek in 20-minute intervals reduces swelling
- **Avoid hot foods and drinks** if you have temperature sensitivity
- **Do not put aspirin directly on the tooth or gum** — this can cause chemical burns
